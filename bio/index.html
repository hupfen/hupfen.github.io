A coworker once described me as "not odd, just Portland odd." I think that sums it up pretty well.

Professional

I've been doing web development, in one form or another, since 2008. My first visible project was a redesign and re-engineering of the site for Signatures, my college's arts and literature magazine. Since then, I've moved from the world of PHP and jQuery to the world of C# and AngularJS — professionally, at least. My own work is more of a MEAN stack.

The bulk of my work has fallen somewhere in the enterprise and SaaS domains. Earlier projects were more on the intranet side, and I have helped build a few e-commerce sites, but the predominant work lately has been targeted at subscribed end users. It's rare I do a marketing-style site for anyone other than myself.

My college degree is in Software Engineering (with a minor in Creative Writing, because why not). I've always applied it to front-end work, but as of late that's been my principle focus. Any back-end work is now relegated to my personal projects — efforts I run both as experiments and as ways of learning more about business and general professional skills.

Personal

Like many programmers, I was a bit of a geek growing up — a bit of video games, a bit of sci-fi. As I've grown up, I've veered more towards music as a point of interest, picking up drums and now playing as part of a post-rock band.

I'm a frequent quizzer and general fan of trivia. I've always found that it provides a broader context on the world and helps fuel creativity. A good knowledge in trivia means a good body of things to pull from. (Plus it's fun to realize you know things.)

For over a decade, off and on, I've been part of the furry community. It's an oft-maligned and usually secretive hobby, but the community has helped me in my most dire times and given me a worldview that seems to continuously pay dividends. I'm not afraid to be a bit of a rabbit, and, at least in Portland, people seem okay with playing along.
